Apple to Spend $30 Billion on U.S.-Made Chips From Broadcom

Apple announced on July 8 a multiyear agreement worth more than $30 billion with Broadcom to design and produce custom wireless-connectivity chips in the United States. It is Apple's largest domestic manufacturing commitment to date and is expected to yield more than 15 billion U.S.-made chips over its life. The deal includes a roughly $1.5 billion expansion of Broadcom's Fort Collins, Colorado facility and runs via long-term contracts through 2031. It forms the biggest single piece of Apple's previously announced $600 billion, four-year U.S. investment plan. Broadcom shares jumped about 5% on the news.

China Says It Has Found Security Vulnerabilities in Anthropic's Claude Code

China's Ministry of Industry and Information Technology warned on July 8 that its cybersecurity platform flagged a "backdoor" vulnerability in Anthropic's Claude Code. It said versions released between April and June (roughly 2.1.91 through 2.1.196) contain a built-in monitoring mechanism that can transmit users' location and identity data to remote servers without consent. An Anthropic engineer characterized the feature as a March experiment meant to prevent account abuse by unauthorized resellers and to detect users tied to Chinese AI labs. In response, Alibaba reportedly ordered employees to stop using Anthropic tools for work starting July 10. The dispute lands amid broader U.S.–China friction over AI tooling and data security.

Nokia's New Act: Supplying the AI Data Center Boom

Nokia has repositioned itself as a key networking supplier for the AI data-center buildout, riding surging demand from AI and cloud customers to its highest stock price in more than 16 years. First-quarter orders from AI and cloud customers reached roughly €1 billion, with revenue from that group up about 49% year over year, led by its optical-networks business serving hyperscalers. On the strength of those results the company raised its full-year outlook for network infrastructure and elevated that unit to a growth segment. It also opened an AI Networking Innovation Lab at its Sunnyvale, California site to co-develop hardware for AI workloads.

AI Giants Are Handing Out Tons of Free Computing Power to Grab Startup Share

OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google are flooding early-stage startups with free compute and token credits to lock them into their ecosystems, competing on customer acquisition rather than price. OpenAI has offered packages such as $2 million in credits for equity plus a $500,000 no-equity tier; Anthropic countered with up to $500,000 (no equity, up sharply from a prior $30,000 offer); and Google Cloud offers up to $500,000 in credits plus early Gemini access. Y Combinator companies are especially targeted, and one founder — Dialogus' Hans Ibarra — said competing offers totaled more than $3 million in cloud and token credits. The strategy relies on lock-in: once a startup builds on one provider, migrating becomes costly and technically hard. It marks an escalation in the platform war for the next generation of AI-native companies.

Microsoft Is Cutting More Than 3,000 Jobs in Xbox Division

Microsoft announced around July 6 that it is cutting roughly 4,800 jobs in total (about 2.1% of its workforce), with the Xbox division bearing the largest share. Xbox will lose about 3,200 positions phased in through July 2027, with roughly 1,600 taking effect immediately. The company also plans to spin off four studios — Compulsion Games, Double Fine, Ninja Theory, and Undead Labs. Xbox CEO Asha Sharma told staff the unit ran at margins three to 10 times lower than comparable platforms and lost 64 cents on every dollar invested in studios. The next-generation console (codenamed Helix, slated for late 2027/2028) remains on schedule.

Big Tech Has Suddenly Flipped on the AI Jobs Wipeout Scenario

After a year of warnings that AI would wipe out large numbers of jobs, tech leaders have shifted to a more optimistic tone emphasizing that humans will stay central to AI-driven work. OpenAI's Sam Altman conceded the industry was "pretty wrong on the social and economic implications," while Anthropic's Dario Amodei — who warned in May 2025 that AI could eliminate half of entry-level jobs — has since highlighted more positive scenarios. An EY-Parthenon survey found the share of CEOs expecting AI to cause significant headcount reductions fell from about 46% in January 2025 to just 20% by May 2026. MIT economist David Autor attributed the shift either to the labor market not moving as fast as expected, or to leaders realizing it is bad business to say your product will destroy the economy. The reversal doesn't necessarily mean the jobs risk is gone — just that the messaging has changed.

Forget Wall Street. Elite Students Are Spending Their Summers on Startup Dreams.

Elite university students are increasingly skipping finance, consulting, and Big Tech internships to launch AI startups, aided by new university-affiliated programs and a shakier entry-level job market. Examples include a Princeton student who turned down a Big Tech offer and a rocket-engineering role to build an AI startup in San Francisco, and Yale sophomore Leïa Ryan, who co-founded Cortex, a knowledge-systems startup for biolabs, instead of taking a biotech job. Supporting hubs include the alumni- and VC-backed Yale Hacker House (15 founders sharing a Nob Hill apartment) and TekTrek, a first-year incubator drawing mainly from MIT, Harvard, and Princeton at a temporary Presidio campus. A big driver is how much cheaper building software has become thanks to LLMs and open-source tools. The trend reflects a broader shift in where ambitious young talent sees the best risk-adjusted upside.

Markets
U.S. Stocks Decline After Trump Says Iran Ceasefire Is Over

President Trump declared the U.S.–Iran ceasefire "over," reigniting fears of regional escalation and threats to oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell about 623 points (1.2%) and the S&P 500 slipped 0.4%, while Germany's DAX dropped 2.2%. Oil surged roughly 6–7%, with Brent crude climbing to about $78.80 a barrel and WTI near $75, after Central Command said U.S. forces had struck more than 80 sites in Iran. The Strait of Hormuz carries roughly a fifth of the world's seaborne oil, so any disruption there feeds directly into inflation fears. Safe-haven assets firmed as investors braced for further military action.

SpaceX Shares Stumble in Nasdaq-100 Debut

SpaceX shares fell about 6.8% on their first day in the Nasdaq-100 (Tuesday, July 7), closing near $149.47 and sliding further to around $148 the next day — below the $150 level where the stock began trading on June 12. The drop disappointed investors who bet that index inclusion would draw guaranteed demand from funds tracking the benchmark, which manage roughly $800 billion in assets. Analysts noted much of that buying may already have been priced in after the stock ran up nearly 6% the prior week. A weak broader tape — the Nasdaq Composite fell 1.2% on semiconductor weakness — added pressure. Still, Wall Street stayed bullish, with Deutsche Bank setting a $255 target citing reusable rockets and satellite-internet growth.

Samsung Fails to Impress, Amazon Issues AI Bonds and Oil Jumps

Samsung Electronics posted a roughly 19-fold (about 1,800%) surge in Q2 operating profit to around $58 billion, but its shares still fell about 7–8% as the results missed lofty AI expectations and sparked profit-taking after a ~150% run this year. The disappointment dragged chip peers lower, with Micron down 4.7% and AMD, Broadcom, and the SMH semiconductor ETF (-3%) all falling. Oil jumped after Iran attacked a Qatari LNG tanker near the Strait of Hormuz, sending Brent up about 3% to $74.16 and WTI up nearly 3% to $70.44. The session's other theme was corporate AI financing, as large tech issuers tapped debt markets to fund infrastructure buildouts. The Nasdaq Composite fell 1.16% to 25,818.69 and the S&P 500 slid 0.45% to 7,503.85.

World's Hottest Market Risks Becoming a Squid Game

The piece refers to South Korea's stock market, the year's best performer, with the KOSPI up roughly 165% over the past year. The concern is that extreme retail speculation could leave small investors holding steep losses if the rally reverses. Retail traders poured about ₩1.61 trillion ($1.1 billion) into leveraged ETFs tied to Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix, and the market logged a record 29,357 volatility-interruption triggers in the first half. Meanwhile foreign investors sold heavily, pulling out on a net basis with roughly $30 billion exiting in June alone. The "Squid Game" framing warns that a euphoric, speculation-driven run rarely ends well for latecomers.

U.S. Stocks Retreat on Worries About AI Trade, Hormuz Fears

All three major U.S. indices closed lower on July 7, led by tech on doubts about the durability of the AI rally. The Nasdaq Composite fell 1.16% to 25,818.69, the S&P 500 dropped 0.45% to 7,503.85, and the Dow lost 0.25% to 52,925.15. The selling began in Asia, where South Korea's Kospi tumbled nearly 5% after Samsung fell almost 7%, and was compounded by a report that China's DeepSeek is developing its own AI chip. Oil rose after Iran struck a Qatari LNG tanker near the Strait of Hormuz — a waterway handling about 20% of the world's oil traffic — after the White House revoked Iran's oil-sales license. Taken together, the session captured the market's two dominant anxieties: stretched AI valuations and Middle East supply risk.

Momenta Global, the Chinese autonomous-driving firm backed by General Motors and Tencent, raised about $751 million (HK$5.89 billion) at HK$295.60 per share, valuing it near $9 billion. It posted a 2025 net loss of 3.46 billion yuan on revenue up 82.1% to 2.41 billion yuan.

A Couple Living on One Income Wants to Buy a Home. Can They Afford It?

A common affordability benchmark is the 28/36 rule: keep housing costs (principal, interest, taxes, insurance, and HOA) at or below 28% of gross monthly income and total debt at or below 36%, though many lenders permit debt-to-income ratios up to about 43–45% on conventional loans. Single-income households are typically advised to stress-test their budget against just one wage, since one salary must cover the mortgage plus emergencies. As a rough 2026 example, a $500,000 home with 20% down at a ~6.5% 30-year fixed rate runs about $2,528/month in principal and interest, requiring roughly $125,000 in annual income to stay within the 28% limit. Beyond the payment, buyers should budget for property taxes, insurance, maintenance, and a healthy emergency fund. On one income, keeping a larger cash cushion matters more because there's no second paycheck to absorb a job loss.

What It's Like to Retire and Start a Business in America

"Encore entrepreneurship" is a real and growing trend: self-employment among Americans 65 and older rose from 13.0% in 2013 to 16.3% in 2023, according to the SBA Office of Advocacy. Adults aged 55–64 now account for more than a quarter of U.S. startup activity. Research cited by AARP finds a 50-year-old founder is roughly 2.8 times more likely to launch a successful startup than a 25-year-old, thanks to deeper experience, networks, and capital. Older founders are typically motivated by purpose, flexibility, legacy, and supplemental income to extend financial security in retirement. Financial planners caution retirees to cap how much retirement savings they put at risk and to keep the venture from jeopardizing essential income.

3 Questions to Ask Yourself About Your First 401(k)

First-time enrollees should weigh three things. First, contribute at least enough to capture the full employer match — a common formula is 100% on the first 3% plus 50% on the next 2%, so contributing 5% earns a 4% match (effectively free money). Second, choose between Roth (contributions taxed now, withdrawals tax-free) and traditional (pre-tax now, taxed in retirement) based on whether you expect higher taxes later. Third, decide how much to contribute against the 2026 limits: $24,500 in employee deferrals, plus an $8,000 catch-up at 50+ (or $11,250 for ages 60–63), with a combined employee-plus-employer cap of $72,000. Note a 2026 rule change: catch-up contributions for those earning over $145,000 must generally be Roth.

Strategy Logs $8.3 Billion Loss as It Sells Off Bitcoin

Strategy (formerly MicroStrategy) reported an $8.32 billion loss on its digital-asset holdings for Q2 2026 amid Bitcoin's sharp decline from its October 2025 peak. In a major reversal from its long-standing buy-and-hold approach, the company sold 3,588 BTC for about $216 million between June 29 and July 5 at average prices near $59,000–$61,000 — below its roughly $75,476 average cost basis. It used the proceeds to cover preferred-stock distributions and rebuild cash reserves. Strategy now holds about 843,775 Bitcoin, still the largest corporate stash. Its shares slipped on the news as investors weighed whether the company's leveraged bitcoin bet is starting to strain.

Trump's Brokerage Accounts Made Big Trades Around 'Liberation Day' Tariffs

A financial disclosure showed investment accounts owned by President Trump made 327 previously undisclosed stock purchases worth as much as $12.8 million on April 8, 2025 — one day before he announced a pause on his April 2 "Liberation Day" tariffs. The buying spree concentrated in mega-cap tech names like Apple, Alphabet, Amazon, Microsoft, and Nvidia, which rebounded sharply after the tariff reversal. That reversal helped send the S&P 500 up roughly 9.5% in one of its best days ever. Trump had posted it was a "GREAT TIME TO BUY!!" the day before the pause. The filing noted late-filing fees for transactions that had not been reported on time, adding to ethics scrutiny of the president's trading.

Stock Funds Rallied 17.1% in a Quarter That Made Investors' Heads Spin

The average diversified U.S. stock fund surged in a whipsaw quarter driven by tariff turmoil. After U.S. stocks fell nearly 12% between April 2 and April 8, hopes for softer trade deals sparked a rally that lifted the market about 25% from April 9 through quarter-end. The broad U.S. market rose more than 11% for the quarter — its best showing since Q3 2023. High-growth and cyclical categories led, with large-growth funds like Vanguard Growth (18.4%) and American Funds Growth Fund of America (18.1%) topping the largest funds. The episode is a reminder that trying to time such swings often backfires, since the sharpest gains clustered in just a few sessions.

Small Stocks Are Having Their Biggest Run in Decades

The Russell 2000 small-cap index has surged roughly 22% in the first half of 2026, its best first half since 1991, as the AI-infrastructure boom broadened beyond mega-cap tech to smaller suppliers. Semiconductor and chip-equipment firms have been the standout winners, accounting for 16 of the index's 50 top performers — names like Aehr Test Systems, Ichor Holdings, and MaxLinear have each rallied more than 400%. The rally has been fueled by Federal Reserve rate cuts, a narrowing earnings gap between small and large caps, and strong technical momentum. From its April 8, 2025 low, the Russell 2000 is up about 55.8% through mid-2026. Small caps had lagged for years, so the rotation marks a notable shift in market leadership.

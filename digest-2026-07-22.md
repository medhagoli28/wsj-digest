**2. **3. The AI Backlash Is Starting to Sting** — Public and local resistance to AI is hardening into a genuine business risk in 2026, driven by anxieties over jobs, energy, and trust. Gallup polling cited by Axios found only 18% of people ages 14 to 29 feel hopeful about AI, while over 70% of Americans (68% of Republicans and 77% of Democrats) say the technology is advancing too quickly. Data-center opposition has been especially acute: local pushback blocked or delayed 75 projects worth roughly $130 billion in the first quarter of 2026 alone—matching all of 2025—as the number of organized opposition groups doubled to 833 across 49 states, per Platformer. On jobs, 71% of professionals surveyed expect AI to eliminate more roles than it creates over the next three years, and AI is now the leading cited reason for tech-sector layoffs. Morgan Stanley analysts warn the backlash is becoming "a binding constraint" on AI buildout that PR campaigns alone cannot resolve. *Sources: [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/17/ai-backlash-polling-sentiment), [Platformer](https://www.platformer.news/ai-backlash-data-centers-jobs-inflation/), [Stanford HAI](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2026-ai-index-report/public-opinion).*

## 💰 Personal Finance

**1. Inside America's Most Generous 401(k) Plans** — A "generous" 401(k) is defined by how much the employer adds on top of your own savings, whether through a dollar-for-dollar match, a richer 2-for-1 match, or automatic non-elective contributions that land even if you contribute nothing; the national average employer match is only about 4.7% of pay, so top-tier plans stand out sharply. Standouts include Visa (2% for every 1% you put in, up to a 10% company contribution for a combined 15%), Boeing and Uber (dollar-for-dollar up to 10% of pay), Southwest Airlines (up to 9.3% with immediate vesting), Amgen (5% automatic plus a full match up to 5%, for 10% total), and General Motors (4% match plus a 6% automatic contribution). For 2026 the IRS raised the employee deferral limit to $24,500, with an $8,000 catch-up at 50+ (and a higher $11,250 catch-up for ages 60-63 under SECURE 2.0), while combined employee-plus-employer contributions can reach $72,000 under IRC Section 415(c). Only the first $360,000 of compensation counts when calculating these contributions. Immediate vesting and eligibility, as offered by firms like Boeing and Southwest, further separate the most generous plans from those that stretch vesting over several years. *Sources: [Kiplinger](https://www.kiplinger.com/retirement/retirement-planning/average-401-k-match-do-you-work-for-a-generous-company), [Carry](https://carry.com/learn/companies-with-biggest-401k-employer-match), [IRS](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/401k-limit-increases-to-24500-for-2026-ira-limit-increases-to-7500).*

**2. The Startup Insiders Who Stash Huge Sums in Tax-Subsidized Retirement Accounts** — Founders and early employees can shelter enormous gains by buying founder shares while they are worth fractions of a penny inside a self-directed Roth IRA, where all future appreciation grows and can be withdrawn tax-free after age 59½; the archetype is PayPal co-founder Peter Thiel, who in 1999 bought roughly 1.7 million shares for about $1,700 in his Roth and, per ProPublica, grew it into a reported $5 billion tax-free by 2019. Higher earners also use the "mega-backdoor Roth," funneling after-tax 401(k) dollars up to the 2026 Section 415(c) ceiling of $72,000 (about $47,500 of after-tax room beyond the $24,500 deferral) and converting them to Roth. Section 1202 QSBS is another shelter: under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, stock issued after July 4, 2025 in a C-corp with up to $75 million in gross assets can exclude the greater of $15 million or 10x basis in gains per issuer (up from $10 million). OBBBA also replaced the all-or-nothing five-year rule with a tiered schedule (50% exclusion at 3 years, 75% at 4 years, 100% at 5 years), though non-excluded gains are taxed at 28%. Solo 401(k)s and self-directed accounts let the self-employed stack these strategies, avoiding nondiscrimination testing but requiring plan documents that explicitly permit after-tax contributions and in-plan Roth conversions. *Sources: [ProPublica](https://www.propublica.org/article/lord-of-the-roths-how-tech-mogul-peter-thiel-turned-a-retirement-account-for-the-middle-class-into-a-5-billion-dollar-tax-free-piggy-bank), [Baker Tilly](https://www.bakertilly.com/insights/changes-to-section-1202-qualified-small-business), [Fidelity](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/personal-finance/mega-backdoor-roth).*

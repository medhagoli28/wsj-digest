SpaceX in Talks to Provide Computing Power for Pentagon's AI Push

SpaceX is in talks with the U.S. Department of Defense to supply data-center computing capacity worth billions of dollars to power AI models across Pentagon operations, according to reporting surfaced July 17, 2026, though the discussions remain preliminary and could still collapse. A deal would push SpaceX's computing business into defense and pit it against incumbent hyperscalers Amazon, Microsoft, Google, and Oracle, which already supply the Pentagon. The effort builds on SpaceX's February 2026 acquisition of Elon Musk's xAI, which folded xAI's AI models and large data-center footprint into the company, and leans on emerging orbital infrastructure like its AI1 satellite program and the Starlink network. SpaceX already holds computing agreements with Google, Anthropic, and Reflection AI. The company argues it can undercut legacy hyperscalers because it built its compute stack alongside its satellite and AI operations rather than through traditional enterprise sales.

Trump Media to Sell Faster Access to President's Social Posts

Trump Media & Technology Group announced it will sell Wall Street firms high-speed access to posts from the 10 most influential Truth Social accounts, including President Donald Trump's, through a new product called "Truth API." The feed is designed to deliver posts "in milliseconds," far faster than a standard Truth Social push notification, targeting algorithmic trading firms "most impacted by the cost of a delay in information." Trump's posts routinely move markets, making low-latency access potentially lucrative for traders, and the company said it had already signed up customers ahead of the planned Aug. 1 launch. The rollout drew sharp criticism, with Sen. Adam Schiff calling it "yet another scheme, this time to save his failing social media business" and some Democrats questioning its legality. Trump owns roughly 41% of Trump Media through a revocable trust.

China's Moonshot AI Releases Model to Challenge Top U.S. Systems

China's Moonshot AI released Kimi K3, a 2.8-trillion-parameter model the company calls the largest open-source AI model in the world, positioning it as a direct challenge to leading proprietary U.S. systems from Anthropic and OpenAI. Benchmarks show K3 running neck-and-neck with the frontier: it ranked first in LMArena's Frontend Code evaluation at 1,679 points and beat Claude Opus 4.8 and GPT-5.5 on coding and general-agent tests, while still trailing Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 and OpenAI's GPT-5.6 on overall performance. The release, reported around July 17, 2026, underscores how Chinese labs are pushing open-weight models to work around U.S. compute export limits. Full model weights are scheduled for public release on July 27, 2026. The launch extends Moonshot's pivot toward open-source begun with Kimi K2 in July 2025.

Databricks Set to Hit $188 Billion Valuation With New Investment From Coatue

Databricks said on July 16, 2026, that it has signed a term sheet for a strategic funding round at a $188 billion valuation, led by existing investor Coatue and expected to close later in the summer. Coatue is anchoring roughly $3 billion in new investment, a jump of about 40% above Databricks' December 2025 valuation, marking the company's second raise of 2026. Databricks said it will use the capital to accelerate its AI strategy, doubling down on its Unity AI Gateway governance layer, its Genie AI "coworker," and Lakebase, a serverless Postgres database built for AI agents. The round cements Databricks' status as one of the most richly valued private companies in AI. Additional new and existing investors are participating alongside Coatue.

Meta Plans to Hire Top Amazon Computing Executive as It Weighs Cloud Push

Meta is set to hire Dave Brown, an AWS senior vice president who is leaving Amazon at the end of July after nearly 19 years, to help oversee its rapidly expanding data-center footprint. Brown will report to Meta's head of infrastructure as the company accelerates AI buildout and weighs launching a cloud computing business, part of a broader "Meta Compute" initiative targeting hundreds of gigawatts of capacity. Meta's flagship Louisiana AI data center is expanding from 2 gigawatts to 5 gigawatts, with investment there alone exceeding $50 billion. CEO Mark Zuckerberg has floated eventually selling surplus computing capacity and AI services if Meta builds more infrastructure than it needs. The company has guided to $125 billion to $145 billion in capital expenditures, much of it aimed at AI data centers.

China's Xi Touts Open-Source AI and Takes a Swipe at U.S. Dominance

Speaking at the World AI Conference in Shanghai on Friday, July 17, 2026, Chinese leader Xi Jinping endorsed open-source AI models and cast China as a champion of openness and equality, implicitly criticizing U.S. moves to protect its lead in AI chips and models. The pitch aligns with China's strategy of expanding open-model partnerships across BRICS, ASEAN, Africa, and Latin America.

IBM CEO Arvind Krishna Has Nowhere to Hide From AI

IBM shares plunged about 25% on July 14, 2026, the company's worst single-day drop on record, surpassing the 23.7% decline of October 19, 1987. The rout followed a preliminary Q2 2026 warning in which IBM guided to roughly $17.2 billion in revenue and $2.93 adjusted EPS, missing consensus estimates of about $17.86 billion and $3.02. CEO Arvind Krishna blamed a "substantial shift" in corporate spending toward AI hardware — servers, storage, and memory — amid supply constraints, pressuring IBM's software and consulting-heavy business, and candidly admitted the company "did not adapt and move quickly enough." The warning rattled the broader software sector, signaling that enterprise budgets are being redirected to AI infrastructure at software's expense. Analysts framed the drop as a reckoning for a company caught flat-footed by the AI capex wave.

Blockbuster Stock Sales Are Threatening to Overwhelm the Bull Market

A rush by some of the world's largest companies to raise cash through equity issuance is putting the multi-year bull market at risk, as analysts warn the surge in new supply echoes the late stages of prior rallies. Recent mega-deals include SpaceX's record IPO, which raised $75 billion at pricing (climbing above $85 billion after the greenshoe overallotment and pushing its valuation past $2 trillion), and Alphabet's roughly $85 billion equity raise earmarked for AI infrastructure, which included a $10 billion private placement to Berkshire Hathaway. On top of those, South Korea's SK Hynix launched a $26 billion-plus sale of American depositary receipts. The core worry is market mechanics: bull markets rarely die of old age or high valuations alone, but they can stall when new issuance overwhelms investors and supply outstrips demand. SpaceX priced at $135 a share and jumped about 19% on debut, closing near $161.

The Boardroom Debate That Led to IBM's Historic 25% Stock Plunge

IBM shares cratered about 25% on Tuesday, July 14, 2026, the worst single-day drop in the company's history, exceeding its 23.7% fall on Oct. 19, 1987, after IBM preannounced weak second-quarter results. The company reported adjusted earnings of $2.93 a share on revenue of $17.2 billion, below Wall Street's expectations of $3.01 a share and $17.86 billion. CEO Arvind Krishna, in an unusual letter to investors, admitted the company had failed to adapt quickly enough, saying numerous large deals failed to close on the expected timelines and that clients had sharply reprioritized capital spending in the final weeks of June, redirecting funds from IBM's software and infrastructure offerings toward hardware such as servers, storage, and memory chips. At least a dozen analysts cut their ratings or targets, with some flagging $213 as a potential support level. The plunge erased tens of billions in market value in a single session.

South Korea Regulator Unveils Curbs on High-Risk ETFs

South Korea's Financial Services Commission announced Thursday, July 16, 2026, that it would suspend new listings of single-stock leveraged ETFs, effective immediately, to curb surging market volatility tied to products linked to Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix. The move came barely seven weeks after the products launched: the FSC approved them on Jan. 30, 2026, and they went live May 27 capped at 2x leverage, quickly generating enormous trading volumes. Regulators will also ban securities firms and asset managers from advertising or marketing the products and will triple the minimum cash deposit for new investments to 30 million won (about $20,000), a requirement slated to take effect Aug. 5. Traders will additionally need to complete a mandatory one-hour investor education session before accessing the products. The FSC had originally framed the leveraged ETFs as a modernization step to keep domestic capital from flowing overseas.

Here's What Is Happening Behind the Market's Swings, in Charts

Mid-July 2026's market turbulence was driven primarily by an unwinding of the AI trade, as chip stocks fell into a bear market on fears that the AI capital-spending spree had pushed valuations beyond what fundamentals could support; the VanEck Semiconductor ETF dropped more than 4% in a session and sold off over 17% for the month. The result was a sharp sector rotation, with capital moving out of high-valuation technology into industrials, financials, and defensive sectors, leaving the S&P 500 down 1.01% at 7,457.69 and the Nasdaq down 1.4% at 25,520.24 on July 17.

The Podcasting Tech Investor Now Busy Hyping Trump Accounts

Trump Accounts, tax-advantaged investment accounts for U.S. children under 18, launched July 4, 2026, with the federal government seeding $1,000 for eligible kids born from 2025 through 2028. Funds must be invested in diversified U.S. equity index funds or ETFs, defaulting to a low-cost S&P 500 fund, and notably crypto is excluded despite heavy promotion from finance-world boosters. A wave of promoters — corporate backers like Dell (matching contributions for employees) and Robinhood, plus celebrities such as Nicki Minaj pledging $150,000–$300,000 for fans — have publicly hyped the program. More than 6 million children have been registered, with about 1.4 million eligible for the $1,000 seed money, and philanthropic pledges have topped $6 billion. Critics warn the enthusiastic promotion oversells modest, market-dependent returns and channels attention toward loosely regulated financial products.

Debt Collectors Chase More Consumers in Court

Debt-collection lawsuits have surged back to pre-pandemic highs across U.S. state courts as collectors ramp up filings against consumers. In Massachusetts, consumer debt cases jumped to roughly 146,000 in 2025, a more than 60% increase over two years, and consumer litigation filings under the FDCPA and FCRA rose sharply through early 2026. Large debt buyers dominate the dockets — LVNV Funding's filings are up about 350% since 2019, and in states like Indiana the top 10 filers now account for a growing share of collection cases. The lawsuits are especially consequential because consumers are represented by attorneys in fewer than 10% of cases (sometimes below 1%), and most defendants never appear in court, leading to frequent default judgments. Advocates say many cases involve borrowers being misled or pressured over old or poorly documented debts.

The Trigger Clause More Couples Want in Their Prenups

A growing number of couples are asking to add "infidelity clauses" (also called no-cheating or lifestyle clauses) to prenuptial agreements, imposing a predetermined financial penalty, asset forfeiture, or loss of spousal support if one spouse has an affair. These are one type of broader lifestyle clause that can also cover matters like household responsibilities, weight, in-law visits, and other personal conduct, with noncompliance tied to financial consequences. Enforceability is the central problem: several no-fault divorce states — including California, Nevada, Iowa, and Hawaii — generally refuse to enforce infidelity clauses, and courts are reluctant to police personal behavior within a marriage. Family lawyers note the clauses often function more as a deterrent or a values statement than an enforceable financial term. They also carry interpersonal risk, since merely proposing one can breed distrust before the wedding.

Why High Credit Card Delinquencies Aren't Showing Up at the Big Banks

National credit-card delinquency stood at about 2.9% in Q1 2026, down from a 3.2% peak in 2024 but still above the roughly 2.6% pre-pandemic level, masking a sharp split by lender. Small-bank card delinquency runs around 6.4% — roughly double the big-bank rate — reflecting a K-shaped credit story in which stress concentrates among lower-income borrowers and smaller lenders. Big banks show stable-to-improving metrics: Bank of America's delinquency rate fell to 1.39% in January 2026 from 1.48% a year earlier, and JPMorgan's Chase Issuance Trust delinquency held near 0.88%. Their higher-income customer bases keep spending and paying balances down, so the deterioration among subprime and fintech-served borrowers doesn't surface in headline big-bank numbers. Industry charge-offs of roughly 3.8% remain off the 4.6% cycle peak but slightly above pre-pandemic norms.

Nearly a Million People Have Left the SAVE Student-Loan Program

Nearly 1 million borrowers have already exited the defunct SAVE repayment plan, which is winding down after nearly two years of litigation left roughly 7 million enrollees in limbo. Starting July 1, 2026, loan servicers began sending 90-day notices instructing SAVE borrowers to choose a new repayment plan or be automatically moved to another; no one will be forced off SAVE before September 29, 2026 at the earliest, with some notices arriving as late as early 2027. Borrowers had been in an involuntary forbearance during the legal fight, but interest resumed accruing on August 1, 2025, so balances have been growing without required payments. Available alternatives include IBR, PAYE, and ICR, plus the new Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP) that opened July 1, 2026. Borrowers pursuing PSLF must switch out of SAVE into a qualifying IDR plan to keep earning credit toward forgiveness.

**2. **1. A Couple Living on One Income Wants to Buy a Home. Can They Afford It?** *(advice — underlying topic)* — A common affordability benchmark is the 28/36 rule: keep housing costs (principal, interest, taxes, insurance, and HOA) at or below 28% of gross monthly income and total debt at or below 36%, though many lenders permit debt-to-income ratios up to about 43–45% on conventional loans. Single-income households are typically advised to stress-test their budget against just one wage, since one salary must cover the mortgage plus emergencies. As a rough 2026 example, a $500,000 home with 20% down at a ~6.5% 30-year fixed rate runs about $2,528/month in principal and interest, requiring roughly $125,000 in annual income to stay within the 28% limit. Beyond the payment, buyers should budget for property taxes, insurance, maintenance, and a healthy emergency fund. On one income, keeping a larger cash cushion matters more because there's no second paycheck to absorb a job loss. *Sources: [Bankrate](https://www.bankrate.com/mortgages/what-is-the-28-36-rule/), [Chase](https://www.chase.com/personal/mortgage/education/buying-a-home/28-36-rule).*

**3. 3 Questions to Ask Yourself About Your First 401(k)** *(advice — underlying topic)* — First-time enrollees should weigh three things. Third, decide how much to contribute against the 2026 limits: $24,500 in employee deferrals, plus an $8,000 catch-up at 50+ (or $11,250 for ages 60–63), with a combined employee-plus-employer cap of $72,000.

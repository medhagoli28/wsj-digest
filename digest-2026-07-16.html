TSMC to Invest a Further $100 Billion in U.S. After AI Fuels Surge in Earnings

On July 16, 2026, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. reported second-quarter net profit jumped roughly 77% year-over-year to a record T$706.6 billion (about $22 billion), beating analyst forecasts of T$632.6 billion and marking its ninth straight quarter of double-digit growth. The company raised its full-year 2026 revenue growth guidance to slightly above 40% in dollar terms, up from an earlier estimate of above 30%, citing AI-driven demand it expects to remain robust through 2030. Alongside the earnings, TSMC reaffirmed plans to pour an additional $100 billion into U.S. manufacturing—building three new fabrication plants, two advanced packaging facilities, and an R&D center in Arizona—on top of its existing $65 billion Phoenix commitment, bringing total planned U.S. investment to roughly $165 billion. Executives framed the expansion as the largest single foreign direct investment in U.S. history.

Sources: Euronews, Yahoo Finance, Communications Today

Mira Murati's AI Startup Releases First Model in Bid to Loosen AI Giants' Grip

On July 15, 2026, Thinking Machines Lab, founded by former OpenAI CTO Mira Murati, released its first AI model, an open-weight system called Inkling, more than a year after the company launched. Inkling is a mixture-of-experts model with 975 billion total parameters that activates only about 41 billion for any given task, and it was trained on 45 trillion tokens spanning text, image, audio, and video, reasoning natively across all four modalities. The company openly acknowledged Inkling is not the most powerful model available, instead emphasizing customizability and efficiency, and it was built from scratch rather than fine-tuned atop another firm's model. Thinking Machines had previously raised a record $2 billion seed round at a $12 billion valuation in 2025—before shipping any product.

Sources: TechCrunch, Axios, Fortune

Young Drivers Are Going 'Psycho' On the Road—and Filming It

Research from IAM RoadSmart found that nearly a third of young drivers aged 18–26 admit to taking photos or video while behind the wheel to post on social media, and 57% of those who filmed went on to upload the footage. Safety experts warn the behavior combines visual, manual, and cognitive distraction: one study found filming while driving raises collision risk roughly 8 times versus talking on a phone, while livestreaming was linked to a 27 times higher crash risk compared with focused driving. The same young-driver cohort also reported overlapping risky habits including texting, driving while fatigued, and in some cases driving under the influence. Lawmakers have begun responding—a bill banning drivers from filming, recording, or broadcasting passed the Massachusetts House in July 2024, and Georgia and Washington State have banned watching videos while driving.

Sources: IAM RoadSmart, AAA Magazine, Bodyshop Magazine

AT&T and Verizon Look Beyond the Family Plan

With the traditional four-line family plan increasingly saturated, AT&T and Verizon are reworking their strategies around bundled household accounts and new single-line and senior segments to sustain growth. AT&T has leaned into fiber-plus-wireless convergence, reporting that nearly 45% of its advanced home internet subscribers also take AT&T wireless, betting that multi-service households churn far less than single-product customers. Verizon, meanwhile, launched its flat-rate Simplicity Plan in June 2026 that charges the same price per line regardless of the number of lines—eliminating the multi-line discount that traditionally rewarded families—and pairs it with a $10-per-perk à la carte add-on system, while also nudging up prices for myPlan customers with five or more lines. Both carriers are simultaneously courting solo and 55-plus subscribers with dedicated pricing (AT&T's nationwide 55+ plan at $40/month for one line; Verizon's Unlimited Welcome 55+ in Florida) as analysts note growth is shifting from selling products to locking in stickier accounts.

Sources: Subscription Insider, SaveOnPhone, WhistleOut

China Wants More Babies—So It's Cracking Down on Chatbot Love Affairs

On July 15, 2026, the Cyberspace Administration of China issued its Interim Measures for the Administration of AI Anthropomorphic Interactive Services, targeting AI companion apps that simulate romantic and emotional relationships. The rules outright ban virtual intimate-relationship services for minors and require that adult-facing chatbots avoid fostering deep emotional dependency. The crackdown is tied to China's demographic crisis: the country recorded just 7.92 million births in 2025, a birth rate of 5.63 per 1,000, and regulators fear digital companionship discourages marriage and childbearing. Major Chinese tech firms including Alibaba and ByteDance have already begun suspending certain chatbot features to comply.

Sources: Crypto Briefing, The News, Digital Trends

Meta Workers Accuse It of Using AI to Conduct Discriminatory Layoffs

On July 14, 2026, 26 current and former Meta employees sued the company in Oakland, California, alleging it used AI systems to "score, rank, and select" workers for layoffs rather than relying on managers' judgment, and that the tools disproportionately harmed employees on medical or family leave. The plaintiffs argue the AI's inputs—performance ratings, calibration scores, productivity metrics, "AI-native" ratings, and AI-token consumption—inherently disadvantage those who were on protected leave or whose output was reduced by a disability. Among the plaintiffs, eight had taken pregnancy-related leave and four had recently been on parental leave; the group is part of the roughly 8,000 employees (about 10% of the workforce) Meta began cutting in May. The suit alleges violations of the Family and Medical Leave Act, the Americans with Disabilities Act, the Pregnancy Discrimination Act, and the Pregnant Workers Fairness Act, while a Meta spokesperson responded that the claims "lack merit" and that decisions "were and are made by people, not AI."

Sources: CNBC, CBS News, Fortune

Apple's AI Tools Get China Approval

On July 15, 2026, the Cyberspace Administration of China officially registered Apple's on-device generative AI service, Apple Intelligence, clearing a major hurdle for its long-delayed launch in the country. Approval came on the back of a deal to integrate Alibaba's Qwen model into Apple's operating systems—iOS, iPadOS, macOS, and visionOS—with additional capabilities drawn from Baidu. The green light arrives at a pivotal moment for Apple in China, where it reported a 24.4% year-over-year increase in iPhone shipments in the second quarter, though regulators did not specify a firm launch date. South Korea Regulator Unveils Curbs on High-Risk ETFs

South Korea's Financial Services Commission announced on Thursday, July 16, 2026, that it will temporarily halt new listings of single-stock leveraged exchange-traded products until markets stabilize, responding to a speculative frenzy in funds tracking Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix. Regulators also plan to raise the minimum deposit for leveraged ETF trading to 30 million won (about $20,300) from 10 million won, effective August 5. The 2x single-stock leveraged ETFs, approved in April 2026 and launched in late May, saw assets balloon from roughly $3 billion to about $9.1 billion within weeks, with retail traders holding about 92%. The FSC chairman said he regretted not blocking the launch, warning the products' side effects had grown significantly. The crackdown followed extreme volatility in Korean chip shares that reverberated through global markets.

Sources: Bloomberg, Korea Herald

Blockbuster Stock Sales Are Threatening to Overwhelm the Bull Market

A record surge of new equity issuance is raising fears that supply could outstrip investor demand and stall a long-running bull market, echoing dynamics seen in the late stages of prior rallies. Among the mega-deals cited are SpaceX's roughly $75 billion public offering, an $85 billion equity raise from Alphabet, and a $26 billion-plus sale of American depositary receipts by South Korea's SK Hynix. Notably, SpaceX's stock recently dipped below its IPO price for the first time, underscoring the strain.

Banks Are on a Goldilocks Run. Don't Assume It Will Last.

Big U.S. banks are riding a "Goldilocks" stretch of falling deposit costs, resilient loan yields, buoyant markets and heavy capital-markets activity, but analysts warn a shift to a neutral-rate regime in 2026 could compress that advantage. In Q2 2026 results reported July 14, JPMorgan posted EPS of $6.14 (vs. $5.85 expected) on revenue of about $58 billion, one of its largest beats, after Q1 net income of $16.5 billion (up 13%) and NII up 9% to $25.5 billion. This opinion column addresses Chicago's deepening public-pension crisis, widely regarded as among the worst in the nation. The city's five main funds — municipal, laborers, police, fire and teachers — carry roughly $53 billion in unfunded liabilities by Equable Institute estimates, more pension debt than 43 (and by some 2024 tallies, 44) U.S. states, exceeding total city employee payroll by more than eightfold. The Trigger Clause More Couples Want in Their Prenups

So-called "trigger" or lifestyle clauses—infidelity provisions, social-media disparagement limits, and even frozen-embryo terms—are increasingly written into prenuptial agreements, especially among younger and creator-economy couples. According to First's 2026 Prenup Report, roughly 53% of engaged and married couples under 45 have signed a prenup as of May 2026, and one analysis found about 41% of Gen Z couples now sign them, some including clauses that assign ownership of social-media handles and influencer income. Infidelity or "no-cheating" clauses tie financial penalties to a spouse straying, but attorneys caution that many states will not enforce them and that proving a breach is difficult. Family-law practitioners generally frame these clauses as conversation-starters about trust and expectations rather than reliably enforceable terms.

Sources: First (Prenup Report 2026), Divorce.law, HelloPrenup

He's 17 Years Old and His Family's Financial Adviser

The story reflects a broader push to build financial literacy in teens, whom experts consider prime candidates for hands-on money lessons once they start earning and asking about household budgets. Research cited by financial institutions finds that parents are the single biggest influence on their children's money habits—outweighing school courses, peers, jobs and media combined—and that early hands-on experience strongly predicts later financial self-efficacy. Common recommendations include opening a bank account and issuing a debit card around age 13, paying commissions for chores rather than a no-strings allowance, and openly involving kids in family budgeting decisions. As of 2026, personal-finance education is increasingly mandated in schools, but advisers stress that day-to-day modeling at home remains the most durable teacher.

Sources: Bank of America Private Bank, Ramsey Solutions, Charles Schwab

Nearly a Million People Have Left the SAVE Student-Loan Program. Here's Where They Went.

The Biden-era SAVE plan, at its peak the largest income-driven repayment program in U.S. history with roughly 8 million enrollees, had fallen to about 7.2 million borrowers by December 2025—a drop of roughly 800,000 after courts blocked the plan and the Education Department declared it unlawful. Interest on SAVE loans resumed accruing August 1, 2025, and beginning July 1, 2026, servicers started sending borrowers 90-day notices to pick a new plan or be moved automatically into a Standard or Tiered Standard plan. Departing borrowers have shifted into Income-Based Repayment (IBR), Pay As You Earn (PAYE), Income-Contingent Repayment (ICR), or the new Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP), which launched July 1, 2026. Borrower advocates expect many to land in IBR, though most will see monthly payments rise compared with SAVE, and the SAVE forbearance is set to end by September 30, 2026.

Sources: U.S. Dept. of Education, NerdWallet, NPR

A Broken Elbow Forces Two Retirees to Ponder Their Future Mobility

An injury like a broken elbow often prompts retirees to confront aging-in-place planning, since falls are the leading cause of injury for adults 65 and older and more than 25% of that group falls each year. Financial and remodeling data for 2026 show a wide cost range: low-cost fixes such as grab bars ($100–$350 installed each), lever door handles, and comfort-height toilets ($400–$1,000) can be done for under $2,000, while a stair lift runs $2,500–$8,000 and a walk-in shower conversion $6,000–$12,000. A full accessible-bathroom remodel costs $8,000–$25,000, and a whole-home retrofit spanning multiple rooms can reach $18,000–$75,000. Contractors report that about 91% of clients now plan accessibility upgrades before they are strictly needed, because building them into a scheduled project is far cheaper than emergency retrofits after an injury.

Sources: ElderLife Financial, CostToRenovate, Retirement Living

Gold's Faithful Think Twice as Prices Slide From Record Highs

Gold hit a record near $5,600 an ounce in January 2026 but has since tumbled, trading around $4,122 on July 9—down roughly 26% year-to-date and nearly 28% off its peak. Analysts attribute the retreat largely to expectations of Federal Reserve rate hikes: with inflation running well above the 2% target, markets priced in about a 72% chance of at least one hike before year-end, after a U.S.-Iran conflict earlier in 2026 pushed energy prices and inflation higher. Higher rates raise the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold and can unwind the catalysts behind the metal's earlier rally, prompting some longtime gold bulls to reconsider. Still, observers note that geopolitical risk, central-bank buying, ETF inflows, and bar-and-coin demand continue to provide underlying support for the market.

Sources: CBS News, Motley Fool, Scottsdale Bullion & Coin

Student-Loan Debt Is Threatening the Best-Laid Retirement Plans

Student debt is increasingly following Americans into their 60s: more than 3 million people age 62 and older now hold federal student loans, up from 1.8 million in 2018, and as of March 2026 some 3.6 million borrowers carried $114.9 billion in Parent PLUS loans. Baby boomers with federal loans owe about $45,000 on average—more than triple the $13,800 owed by borrowers 24 and under—while the 50-to-61 cohort averages the highest balance at $51,110, and 22.4% of balances for those 50-plus are 90-plus days delinquent. A Fidelity analysis found workers over 50 with student debt hold roughly 30% less in retirement savings ($153,000 vs. $221,000). The CFPB estimates about 452,000 Social Security recipients are in default and at risk of having up to 15% of their monthly benefit garnished, a striking figure given the average benefit of about $2,071.

Sources: CNBC, AARP Public Policy Institute, Bankrate

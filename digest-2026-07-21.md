**3. AliExpress Fined $629 Million by EU Over Product-Safety Concerns** — The European Commission on July 20, 2026 fined Alibaba's AliExpress 550 million euros (about $629 million), the largest penalty ever imposed under the EU's Digital Services Act, for failing to curb the sale of unsafe and counterfeit products. Regulators found the marketplace inadequately assessed and mitigated risks tied to illegal goods, letting counterfeit items, unsafe toys and dangerous cosmetics linger, while its recommendation algorithms actively amplified and pushed those listings toward shoppers. The action follows a June 2025 preliminary finding that AliExpress had breached the DSA over systemic risks. The company now has until October 20 to submit an action plan, with further penalties possible if the Commission judges its remedies inadequate in December; AliExpress said it would appeal, calling the fine disproportionate. *Sources: [Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/07/20/european-commission-aliexpress-629-million-fine-temu/), [UPI](https://www.upi.com/Top_News/World-News/2026/07/20/eu-commission-fines-aliexpress-record-digital-services-act/1581784569025/), [Honolulu Star-Advertiser](https://www.staradvertiser.com/2026/07/20/breaking-news/aliexpress-hit-with-record-629m-eu-fine-over-illegal-products/).*

**4. The Tax Strategy for People Suffering From Stock-Market Success** — Investors sitting on large, highly appreciated concentrated positions — often a single winning stock — face steep federal long-term capital gains taxes of up to 20% plus the 3.8% net investment income tax, prompting a menu of tax-efficient diversification strategies. Exchange funds let investors pool a concentrated stake into a diversified portfolio without an immediate taxable event, but require roughly a seven-year lock-up and a portion held in illiquid assets. Direct indexing surrounds the concentrated holding with index constituents while harvesting losses to offset gains as exposure is trimmed, and charitable remainder trusts (CRTs) let donors contribute appreciated stock, sell it tax-free inside the trust, take income for a term, and claim a deduction for the charitable remainder's present value. Advisers also note a new wrinkle: beginning in 2026, itemized charitable deductions are subject to a 0.5%-of-AGI floor, which can make "bunching" gifts and outright donations of appreciated shares more attractive, typically executed as a coordinated multi-year sequence rather than a single move. *Sources: [Fidelity](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/wealth-management-insights/diversify-concentrated-positions), [Kiplinger](https://www.kiplinger.com/investing/tax-efficient-ways-to-ditch-concentrated-stock-holdings), [Advisor Perspectives](https://www.advisorperspectives.com/articles/2025/12/15/tax-efficient-ways-diversify-concentrated-positions).*

## 💰 Personal Finance

**1. The Man Who Runs the IRS Spied on Colleagues When He Worked at JPMorgan** — The figure at the center is Frank Bisignano, who as of July 2026 holds a newly created IRS "Chief Executive Officer" role running the agency's day-to-day operations while also serving as Social Security Administration Commissioner; the Senate-confirmed IRS Commissioner post has been vacant since Scott Bessent's Vacancies Act authority expired March 6, 2026. Reporting summarized by non-WSJ outlets says that during his tenure as JPMorgan's co-chief operating officer over a decade ago, Bisignano sponsored a security program that used as many as 120 Palantir "forward-deployed engineers" to vacuum up executives' and employees' emails, browser histories, GPS data from company smartphones, printer/download activity, and transcripts of recorded phone calls. The reporting alleges his aim was to keep tight control over staff and undermine internal rivals, and that matters came to a head around 2013 when other JPMorgan executives discovered they were being monitored. Bisignano's attorney flatly denies the claims, stating he "never directed anyone to look through any employee's or executive's communications or engaged in any form of surveillance," and Democratic senators have separately attacked the IRS "CEO" title as an unauthorized job created to bypass Senate confirmation. *Sources: [Newser](https://www.newser.com/story/393197/wsj-irs-chief-once-spied-on-his-fellow-bank-execs.html), [Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/sites/kellyphillipserb/2026/03/11/tax-season-question-whos-running-the-struggling-irs/), [The Hill](https://thehill.com/business/5540374-treasury-announces-new-irs-leader/).*

**2. The Massive Supply Deals Feeding the AI Frenzy Are No Sure Thing** — The AI buildout rests on a web of interlocking supply and financing commitments now estimated in the hundreds of billions to over $1 trillion: OpenAI agreed to pay Oracle roughly $300 billion over five years (about 4.5 gigawatts of capacity, starting 2027) in what was called the largest cloud deal ever, while Nvidia signed a letter of intent to invest up to $100 billion in OpenAI in exchange for non-voting shares, with OpenAI pledging to spend that cash on Nvidia chips. Additional layers include CoreWeave's up-to-$22.4 billion infrastructure commitment to OpenAI (raised from $11.9 billion) and Nvidia's agreement to backstop up to $6.3 billion of CoreWeave's unsold capacity through 2032, plus separate AMD and Microsoft arrangements. Critics warn this "circular financing," where Nvidia funds customers who then buy its chips and cloud providers that also buy its GPUs, creates skewed incentives reminiscent of the dot-com-era vendor-financing collapse. The core uncertainty is demand risk: if AI revenue fails to match lofty projections, or if a single link breaks (e.g., Nvidia deciding OpenAI is too risky to fund), the capital flow could stall, magnifying losses across the chain. *Sources: [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/10/15/a-guide-to-1-trillion-worth-of-ai-deals-between-openai-nvidia.html), [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/business/economy/openai-nvidia-amd-deals-risks-rcna234806), [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2026-ai-circular-deals/).*

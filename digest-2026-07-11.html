The White House Made Fixing Intel Its Pet Project. It's Working.

The Trump administration has turned Intel's revival into a signature industrial-policy effort, converting roughly $9 billion in federal CHIPS grants into a 10% equity stake that made Washington the company's largest shareholder. Beyond the equity, President Trump and Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick have run a customer-pressure campaign: Apple now plans to have Intel manufacture chips for certain products, Nvidia invested about $5 billion, and Google Cloud placed large orders for Intel's Xeon CPUs. The financial picture is mending — Q1 2026 data-center revenue rose 22% year over year to $5.1 billion, though Intel still posted a net loss — and the stock has more than quadrupled since Lip-Bu Tan became CEO in March 2025. By late April 2026, the government's stake had jumped roughly 300% to about $36 billion on the share rally.

Sources: Investing.com, PBS NewsHour, Bloomberg

Apple Sues OpenAI, Alleging It Stole Trade Secrets

Apple filed suit against OpenAI in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California on July 10, 2026, alleging a scheme to steal trade secrets tied to unreleased hardware, including technical specifications, engineering presentations, component and vendor selection processes, and a proprietary metal-finishing technique. The complaint names two former Apple employees now at OpenAI: Tang Tan, OpenAI's chief hardware officer who spent 24 years at Apple leading iPhone and Apple Watch product design, and Chang Liu, a senior systems electrical engineer accused of failing to return an Apple laptop and downloading confidential documents. Apple also named io Products — the design firm OpenAI acquired from Jony Ive for $6.5 billion in 2025 — and is seeking an injunction barring OpenAI from using or disclosing the secrets plus return of confidential materials. OpenAI rejected the claims, saying "We have no interest in other companies' trade secrets" and that it is focused on "building innovative technology."

Sources: TechCrunch, CNBC, Fortune

Apple to Spend $30 Billion on U.S.-Made Chips From Broadcom

Apple CEO Tim Cook and Broadcom CEO Hock Tan announced a multiyear agreement on July 8, 2026, under which Apple will spend more than $30 billion to produce over 15 billion U.S.-made chips through at least 2031. Broadcom will design and manufacture custom silicon "for a wide range of Apple products," with $1.5 billion earmarked to upgrade a Broadcom facility in Fort Collins, Colorado, that makes advanced radio-frequency components for Wi-Fi, cellular, and Bluetooth connectivity. It is the largest single commitment Apple has made under its American Manufacturing Program and forms part of the company's broader $600 billion, four-year U.S. investment pledge. The deal deepens one of the semiconductor industry's most strategically important supplier relationships as Washington presses tech giants to reshore chip production.

Sources: CNN Business, CNBC, Apple Newsroom

China Successfully Launches Reusable Rocket in a Win for Space Program

China recovered the first stage of its Long March-10B carrier rocket during an orbital launch from the Wenchang commercial spaceport in Hainan on July 10, 2026, a breakthrough in its race to match SpaceX on reusable technology. About six minutes after stage separation, the roughly 760-ton, five-meter-diameter first stage returned vertically and was captured in a net strung across a sea-based recovery platform — the world's first net-capture recovery of an orbital-class booster and China's first maritime booster recovery. The feat makes China only the second nation ever to recover an orbital-class booster, and officials framed it as filling a domestic gap that will sharply cut commercial launch costs. China plans to refly the very same first stage before the end of the year.

Sources: CGTN, Space.com, CNN

Meta Failed to Protect Users From Addictive Apps, EU Says

The European Commission issued a preliminary finding on July 10, 2026, that Meta's Facebook and Instagram breach the Digital Services Act through "addictive" design choices, singling out infinite scroll, autoplay, push notifications, and engagement-maximizing recommender systems. The Commission said Meta failed to adequately assess the risks these features pose to users' physical and mental wellbeing — particularly minors and vulnerable adults — and that its existing mitigations fell short. Brussels wants Meta to disable autoplay and infinite scroll by default, add effective screen-time breaks, and retune its recommendations away from relentless engagement. If confirmed after Meta responds, the company faces a fine of up to 6% of worldwide annual turnover — more than $12.5 billion against its 2025 revenue of about $200.97 billion.

Sources: European Commission, TechCrunch, CNBC

The Mind Game That Investors Can't Stop Playing

The market-timing impulse — trying to sell before drops and buy back before rebounds — is one of the most persistent and costly behavioral traps in investing. It is rooted in loss aversion, the tendency identified by psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky in which the pain of a loss is roughly twice as powerful as the pleasure of an equivalent gain, pushing investors to panic-sell in downturns and chase rallies at the top. Research suggests emotional decision-making shaves 1–2 percentage points off returns annually, and studies of the "behavior gap" find the average investor earns only about 3–4% versus roughly 10% for the market itself. Because the market's best days often cluster right after its worst, investors who flee downturns tend to miss the recovery, locking in losses that compound over time.

Sources: Morgan Stanley, Allworth Financial, Berger Financial Group

A Couple Living on One Income Wants to Buy a Home. Can They Afford It?

Buying a home on a single income turns on the classic affordability math: the 28/36 rule, which says housing costs should stay under 28% of gross monthly income and total debt payments under 36%. Lenders in 2026 are more flexible in practice — most conventional loans allow debt-to-income ratios up to 50% and FHA loans up to about 56.9% — but stretching to those limits leaves little cushion. The backdrop is tough for one-earner households: the median new home runs roughly $413,500, 30-year fixed rates hover around 6.1%, and the median U.S. household income is about $83,150. Advisers stress that a single-income family carrying a car payment or high state taxes can feel "underwater" at the same 30% housing ratio that a dual-income household absorbs comfortably, so building an emergency fund and minimizing other debt matters more than hitting a rule of thumb.

Sources: Hudson Valley Credit Union, AmeriSave, Zillow

Circle Receives Approval to Launch Crypto-Focused Bank

Stablecoin issuer Circle won final approval from the U.S. Office of the Comptroller of the Currency on July 10, 2026, to establish First National Digital Currency Bank, N.A., becoming the first stablecoin issuer to secure a national trust-bank charter. The new entity will operate under direct federal banking oversight, initially providing digital-asset custody for institutional clients and eventually managing the reserves backing USDC, the world's largest regulated stablecoin. Until now, the cash and short-term Treasurys behind USDC have been held by third-party banking partners; the charter lets Circle bring those multibillion-dollar reserves under its own federally regulated custody. Investors cheered the milestone, sending Circle shares higher on the news.

Why White-Collar Men Are Obsessed With the Perfect Lawn

The manicured American lawn is less about grass than about status, control, and belonging — a cultural fixation with deep economic roots. Americans pour an estimated $40–60 billion a year into the turfgrass industry, and lawns and other managed turf cover more than 40 million acres of the continental U.S., an area roughly three times larger than any single irrigated crop. The obsession is historically shallow: imported from European aristocratic estates, it spread widely only after World War II, when federally financed low-cost mortgages fueled tract-housing suburbs whose builders used lawns to signal middle-class arrival. Psychologists note that a lush, tidy yard functions as a public barometer of a household's success and stability, which helps explain why time-strapped professionals treat weekend mowing and edging as a point of pride.

Sources: Noema Magazine, Scientific American, Freakonomics

Alibaba Says New AI Model Is Just Second to Anthropic's Fable 5** — At the World AI Conference in Shanghai (around July 19-20, 2026), Alibaba previewed Qwen3.8 (available as Qwen3.8-Max-Preview), a sparse Mixture-of-Experts model with roughly 2.4 trillion parameters that the company calls "second only" to Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 among frontier models. **3. Pastors Using AI to Help Write Sermons Grapple With Where to Draw the Line** — A growing share of clergy, roughly a quarter of pastors (up from about 12% in 2024), now use AI tools to assist sermon preparation, prompting debate over acceptable limits. The emerging consensus treats AI as a research and brainstorming partner (surfacing commentary, sketching outlines, exploring theological angles or pastoral-care questions) rather than as the voice in the pulpit. Critics warn that outsourcing the work risks eroding the pastoral relationship, arguing an algorithm cannot know a congregation's miscarriages, divorces, or abuse, nor "preach through tears," and one survey found 79% of pastors worry about AI substituting for God. Prominent voices have drawn firm lines: Pope Leo urged priests to resist "the temptation to prepare homilies with artificial intelligence." The tension is framed as fundamentally theological, about authenticity and lived experience, not merely technological. *Sources: [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2025/07/17/nx-s1-5468637/clergy-grapple-with-the-ethics-of-using-ai-to-write-sermons), [Deseret News](https://www.deseret.com/faith/2026/03/04/ai-and-religion-pope-leo-homilies/), [The Presbyterian Outlook](https://pres-outlook.org/2025/08/are-ai-sermons-ethical-clergy-consider-where-to-draw-the-line/).*

## 💰 Personal Finance

**1. The Trigger Clause More Couples Want in Their Prenups** — Prenups have surged among younger couples, with a 2023 Harris Poll finding roughly 41% of Gen Z and 47% of millennials who were engaged or married had one, versus about 20% of married couples overall, and LegalShield data showing millennials are roughly 10 times likelier than baby boomers to sign one. Within this boom, attorneys report rising demand for "lifestyle" or "trigger" clauses that govern conduct during the marriage rather than just dividing assets afterward, covering things like household duties, social-media behavior, weight, in-law time, and pet custody. The most talked-about variety is the infidelity or "no-cheating" clause, which imposes a financial penalty (for example, a larger settlement or lump-sum payment) if a spouse is unfaithful. Enforceability is uneven: because prenups are governed by contract law, courts can strike clauses deemed unconscionable or against public policy, and no-fault divorce states such as California, Nevada, Iowa, and Hawaii generally refuse to enforce infidelity provisions. Drivers cited include diminished stigma around divorce, greater financial transparency, and student-debt concerns, with surveys showing about 77% of those carrying student debt open to a prenup. *Sources: [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/02/27/gen-z-is-embracing-the-prenup-says-family-law-attorney.html), [Newsweek](https://www.newsweek.com/millennials-are-10-times-more-likely-than-baby-boomers-to-get-a-prenup-11050556), [HerMoney](https://hermoney.com/connect/marriage/consider-an-infidelity-clause-in-your-prenup/).*

**2. Why High Credit Card Delinquencies Aren't Showing Up at the Big Banks** — U.S. credit card debt hit a record $1.25 trillion in Q1 2026 per the New York Fed, and serious delinquencies (90+ days past due) plateaued near a 15-year high, driven mostly by younger, lower-income, and subprime borrowers rather than prime customers. The headline point is a K-shaped split by lender: JPMorgan Chase's 30-day-plus card delinquency rate was just 1.68% in Q1 (up from 1.09% a year earlier), while subprime- and retail-card-heavy specialists ran far higher — Capital One's domestic card delinquencies were about 3.70% as of March 31, and Synchrony's 30-plus rate was 4.52% (actually down from 4.74% a year prior). Post-2008 underwriting tightening pushed the big money-center banks toward prime and super-prime borrowers, so the stress is concentrated at monoline and near-prime lenders rather than the diversified megabanks whose fee income also masks consumer credit pain. Analysts warn a proposed federal cap on credit card interest rates (floated by President Trump in January 2026) could make subprime lending unprofitable and cut off access for those borrowers. In short, the national average understates a genuine subprime problem sitting largely outside the biggest banks' reported numbers. *Sources: [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/12/new-york-fed-credit-card-debt-stands-at-1point25-trillion.html), [Bankrate](https://www.bankrate.com/credit-cards/news/whats-new-with-delinquencies-credit-cards-other-loans/), [Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/sites/mayrarodriguezvalladares/2026/07/16/wall-streets-big-eight-fee-boom-masks-rising-consumer-credit-pain/).*

**3. Gold's Faithful Think Twice as Prices Slide From Record Highs** — Gold set an all-time high of about $5,586 per ounce on January 29, 2026, capping a rally that had blown through the $3,000, $4,000, and $5,000 milestones, before reversing sharply. By July, prices had fallen more than 25% from that peak, hovering around $4,000 an ounce, and the metal posted roughly a 16% drop in the quarter ended June 30 — its worst quarter since Q2 2013. The pullback is largely driven by expectations that the Federal Reserve will hold rates "higher for longer," which hurts non-yielding gold; the selloff accelerated after President Trump named Kevin Warsh to lead the Fed, a pick markets read as less inclined to cut rates. A firmer dollar and even rising odds of a rate hike added pressure, pushing gold to seven-month lows in June. Longtime gold bulls are now reassessing, though some analysts frame the retreat as a buying opportunity and note continued central-bank demand. *Sources: [CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/golds-price-down-25-percent-what-investors-should-do-next/), [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/01/gold-prices-fall-worst-quarter-interest-rates-bullion-precious-metals.html), [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/29/gold-nears-5600-as-safe-haven-rush-intensifies-silver-blazes-past-120.html).*

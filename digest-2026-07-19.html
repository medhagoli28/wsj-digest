IBM's Big Blue Blues
IBM shares cratered about 25% on July 14, 2026 — the stock's worst single-day drop on record — after the company issued a preliminary Q2 warning, guiding to roughly $17.2 billion in revenue and $2.93 adjusted EPS versus consensus of about $17.86 billion and $3.02. CEO Arvind Krishna said IBM "did not anticipate the magnitude of the capex reprioritization," as enterprise clients diverted spending in late June toward supply-constrained AI servers, storage, and memory instead of IBM mainframes and software. By segment, infrastructure fell 7%, while software rose 5% and consulting was up just 1%, underscoring how the AI buildout is siphoning dollars away from IBM's legacy franchises. The warning echoed a more than 20% slide in February 2026 driven by fears that advanced AI coding models could automate COBOL modernization and erode IBM's high-margin consulting and mainframe ecosystem. Analysts framed the plunge as a signal of a broader shift in how enterprises allocate IT budgets amid the AI infrastructure crunch.

IBM CEO Arvind Krishna Has Nowhere to Hide From AI
After IBM's roughly 25% crash on July 14, 2026, CEO Arvind Krishna faced pointed scrutiny over whether AI is now hollowing out IBM's core businesses rather than expanding them. Krishna admitted the company "faltered" and "did not adapt and move quickly enough," telling CNBC that clients were pausing new cybersecurity and consulting deals — partly citing pressure from advanced AI models — while reallocating capex toward supply-constrained AI hardware. The dynamic is doubly painful because AI both diverts customer spending away from IBM mainframes and software and threatens the high-margin consulting and legacy-modernization revenue that has anchored Krishna's turnaround strategy. The preliminary Q2 guide of about $17.2 billion in revenue and $2.93 adjusted EPS missed Wall Street targets, and infrastructure revenue fell 7%. Commentators noted Krishna, who has led IBM since 2020 and bet heavily on hybrid cloud, watsonx AI, and quantum, now has little room to argue IBM is a clear AI winner.

Exclusive | AI Chip Startup Etched Is in Talks for $20 Billion Valuation
San Jose-based AI chip startup Etched is in talks to raise capital at a valuation of roughly $20 billion — about quadruple its prior valuation — in a round reportedly led by existing investor Jane Street, as investors hunt for alternatives to Nvidia. In an unusual back-to-back structure, Etched is simultaneously raising a separate round led by Sequoia Capital at around a $10 billion valuation, neither of which has closed. Founded in 2022 by Harvard dropouts Gavin Uberti, Chris Zhu, and Robert Wachen, Etched builds specialized chips (its "Sohu" transformer ASIC) optimized for AI inference rather than general-purpose GPUs. The company recently exited stealth citing about $1 billion in customer interest and roughly $800 million raised, though large-scale deliveries have not yet begun. Earlier backers include Peter Thiel, Ribbit Capital, Stripes, and Primary Venture Partners.

Exclusive | SpaceX in Talks to Provide Computing Power for Pentagon's AI Push
Elon Musk's SpaceX is in talks with the U.S. Defense Department to provide data-center capacity for running AI models, in an arrangement that could be worth up to several billion dollars, according to reports surfacing around July 17, 2026. The deal would route through SpaceX's Starshield division, which already handles secure satellite communications and operates missile-tracking satellites for the government. If completed, it would push SpaceX into the government AI-cloud market alongside incumbents Amazon, Microsoft, Google, and Oracle, who already supply computing capacity to the Pentagon. The move signals SpaceX's ambition to expand beyond launch and satellite services into defense-grade AI infrastructure amid the Pentagon's broader AI adoption push. The talks were reported as preliminary, with terms not finalized.

Nvidia, Challenged by Apple, Narrowly Retains Wall Street's Crown
Apple briefly overtook Nvidia as the world's most valuable company on July 17, 2026, reaching a market cap of about $4.88 trillion versus Nvidia's roughly $4.86 trillion as chip stocks slid and Nvidia fell around 3.5%. By the close of the week, however, Nvidia edged back ahead, finishing at approximately $4.912 trillion against Apple's $4.901 trillion to narrowly retain the top spot. The tight race reflected growing investor caution about lofty AI valuations weighing on Nvidia, even as Apple rallied on renewed optimism. The two companies have traded the crown repeatedly, with each now valued near the unprecedented $4.9 trillion mark. Analysts framed the episode as a sign that Nvidia's long, uninterrupted reign at the top is facing its most serious challenge yet.

They Call Her 'the Assassin' on Wall Street—and She Has a New Target
The "Assassin" is Fahmi Quadir, founder and CIO of New York short-selling firm Safkhet Capital, who built her reputation with high-profile bets against Valeant Pharmaceuticals and German fintech Wirecard (which collapsed in a 2020 fraud scandal). In May 2026 Quadir revealed a strategic pivot: rather than shorting stocks, she is launching a new long-only activist fund targeting South Korean equities, one of 2026's hottest markets. She plans to focus on smaller, domestically oriented companies with entrenched governance issues, using "long shareholder activism" to unlock value. Korea has historically been inhospitable to activists because of the family-controlled conglomerates (chaebols), complex cross-shareholdings, and high inheritance taxes that depress the so-called "Korea discount," though recent regulatory reforms aim to boost shareholder returns. Her debut as an activist there is planned for summer 2026.

The Boardroom Debate That Led to IBM's Historic 25% Stock Plunge
IBM shares cratered about 25% on Tuesday, July 14, 2026, the company's worst single-day drop on record—surpassing the 23.7% fall of Oct. 19, 1987—after it pre-announced disappointing preliminary second-quarter results. IBM reported adjusted earnings of roughly $2.93 a share on revenue of $17.2 billion, missing analyst expectations of about $3.01 EPS and $17.86 billion in revenue, and erasing roughly $67 billion in market value. CEO Arvind Krishna attributed the shortfall to weakness in software and infrastructure as a severe memory-chip shortage drove prices up and pushed clients to prioritize hardware such as servers and storage, deferring software, consulting, and mainframe purchases. The warning fed a broader debate over IBM's AI-capex-driven strategy and revenue mix. IBM said it would report full second-quarter results on July 22, 2026.

U.S. Chip Stocks Extend Slide; Netflix Tumbles on Growth Warning
U.S. chip stocks extended their selloff on Friday, July 17, 2026, as investors reassessed returns on AI infrastructure spending, with the VanEck Semiconductor ETF falling more than 3% and Micron dropping about 4.7%. Netflix shares tumbled roughly 8.6% after the streaming giant issued weaker-than-expected guidance, forecasting third-quarter revenue near $12.86 billion versus the roughly $13 billion consensus and EPS around 82 cents against 84 cents expected. Netflix also said it would reduce the frequency of its viewing-hours and engagement data disclosures starting in 2027, raising questions about growth momentum. The S&P 500 fell about 0.7% as the combination of chip weakness and Netflix's miss extended a global tech selloff. The rout spread overseas, with Japan's Nikkei 225 down 4.03% and South Korea's KOSPI dropping 6.37%.

What to Know About the Chinese AI Models Rattling U.S. Stocks
On Friday, July 17, 2026, a wave of powerful new Chinese AI models sent U.S. tech stocks tumbling, with the Nasdaq falling about 1.4%, the S&P 500 down 1%, and the Dow off 0.77%. The trigger was Moonshot AI's launch of its open-source Kimi K3 model, which the company claims narrows the performance gap with leading U.S. systems like Anthropic's Claude, stoking fears of another low-cost "DeepSeek moment" (echoing January 2025). A closely watched semiconductor index fell 1.6%, leaving it down about 20% from its late-June record and in a technical bear market; Nvidia dropped 3.4%, AMD 4.9%, and Applied Materials 6.5%. Investors worry that cheap, efficient open-source models could undermine the return-on-investment case for massive U.S. AI infrastructure spending and dent chipmaker revenue forecasts. Some semiconductor stocks trimmed losses later as bargain-hunters bought the dip.

Treasury Yields Slip Amid Solid Indicators, Middle East Tension
U.S. Treasury yields slipped on Friday, July 17, 2026, as investors weighed resilient domestic economic data against fresh Middle East strikes. The benchmark 10-year yield fell more than 4 basis points to about 4.525%, while the 2-year yield shed 3 basis points to 4.122% and the 30-year eased to 5.063%. Softer inflation readings supported the move, with both consumer and producer inflation declining in June and the University of Michigan survey showing inflation expectations falling for a second straight month. At the same time, renewed U.S.-Iran tensions kept a geopolitical bid under bonds, with commercial vessels avoiding the Strait of Hormuz and WTI crude up 1.9% to about $80.44 a barrel. The mix of cooling inflation and safe-haven demand amid the conflict pulled yields lower.

Here's What Is Happening Behind the Market's Swings, in Charts
Mid-July 2026 market volatility was driven primarily by an intensifying semiconductor selloff, with a key chip gauge sinking about 20% from its late-June record into bear-market territory during its worst week since April 2025 and wiping out an estimated $1.3 trillion in value across names like Intel, Micron, and AMD. The core worry is AI-spending sustainability: the four largest U.S. hyperscalers are guiding to roughly $725 billion in combined 2026 capital expenditure, up about 77% from around $410 billion in 2025, with capex growth outpacing cloud-revenue growth and raising return-on-investment doubts. That prompted a sharp rotation out of high-valuation tech and into industrials, financials, and defensives—the VanEck Semiconductor ETF plunged 5.1% in one session even as the Dow pushed above 53,000. Earnings added crosscurrents: UnitedHealth rose about 1.2% after beating and raising guidance, while Netflix slid roughly 9% on a weak Q3 forecast. Fears of a hawkish Fed and stretched, dot-com-era valuations amplified the swings.

This Shortcut to Private-Equity Riches Is Minting Young Millionaires
The private-equity boom is generating outsized wealth for young professionals primarily through carried interest, the roughly 20% share of fund profits paid to managers after limited partners recover their capital plus a preferred return (commonly an ~8% hurdle). Mega-fund associates already earn $325k-$450k in all-in cash, VPs $450k-$700k, and senior partners $900k-$2M+ before carry, while European lower-tier partners can see carried-interest payouts up to roughly €24m ($29m). A major accelerant is the secondaries and continuation-fund boom: secondary transaction volume topped $225 billion in 2025 (up over 40% year over year), with continuation vehicles making up 89% of GP-led volume and about 43% of the total market, letting GPs crystallize carry early on their best assets. Retail and 401(k) money is now flooding in after President Trump's August 2025 executive order easing access to private equity, real estate and crypto in the $14 trillion defined-contribution market, with Deloitte projecting one in six U.S. retail investor funds will have meaningful private-capital exposure by 2030. Insiders forecast secondaries alone could hit $400 billion by 2030, extending the wealth-creation runway for young PE professionals whose carry can compound into tens of millions over a career.

A Summer Reading List — The Intelligent Investor
This evergreen advice column (which shares its name with Benjamin Graham's 1949 classic, The Intelligent Investor, now edited with commentary by columnist Jason Zweig) points readers toward foundational investing books and timeless principles for summer reading. Graham's text, which Warren Buffett called "by far the best book about investing ever written," popularized value investing, the margin of safety, and the parable of "Mr. Market" to teach discipline against emotional decision-making; Buffett credited Graham as a formative influence on his career. Other perennially recommended titles include Burton Malkiel's A Random Walk Down Wall Street, John Bogle's The Little Book of Common Sense Investing (championing low-cost index funds), Charlie Munger's Poor Charlie's Almanack, and Morgan Housel's The Psychology of Money. The common through-lines are evergreen: buy undervalued assets with a margin of safety, keep costs low, diversify, invest for the long term, and control behavioral impulses rather than chasing market timing. These principles endure precisely because they are cheap to follow and largely immune to short-term market noise, making classic investing literature a reliable summer refresher for individual investors.
